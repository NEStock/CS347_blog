During this past week I have spent much time brainstorming and gathering ideas for my project 1 website. The first step in this process was wireframing. I had heard of wireframing before, but in all honesty I had no idea what it actually meant to create a wireframe. In this blog post, I will describe my experience wireframing.

The first thing I wanted to know was what actually is wireframing? After doing a bit of preliminary research, I found the following two guides to be a very helpful start: [1] What Exactly Is Wireframing? A Guide To Wireframes and [2] How I Learned to Create a Wireframe: 6 Easy Steps to Follow

According to [1], wireframing is "a two-dimensional skeletal outline of a webpage or app" which "a clear overview of the page structure, layout, information architecture, user flow, functionality, and intended behaviors". From [2], I learned that wireframing should focus more on UX rather than design; "UX is all about, Who is my audience, and how can I make experience of using my site as seamless and enjoyable as possible for them?". Keeping this in mind, I began by writing out information about my client.

Women In Technology is a JMU student organization whose focus is to encourage women to pursue STEM fields and support them both socially and professionally. Some of the client website's needs are as follows:

  • To attract students to join the organizaion
  • To attract professional organizations to present to the club
  • To give an overview of the organization
  • To show off the organizaion's accomplishments
  • To direct visitors to our social media
  • To collect new contact information for potential members

Keeping in mind the client's needs, I developed a list of pages the website would need and what information should be contained in each page. Once I had these lists completed, I was ready to start designing the page layouts with pencil and paper. After reviewing my designs and selecting the best one, I began desiging the layout using HTML placeholders and css.
